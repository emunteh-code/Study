## Difference between academic verification and reuse permission

Academic verification establishes that a claim is supported by an inspectable source. It does not permit copying wording, translating it, reproducing a diagram, reusing an exercise, or publishing a source PDF.

Reuse permission concerns protected expression, selection, arrangement, visual presentation, and applicable licence terms. Attribution does not replace permission, factual accuracy does not replace clearance, and public accessibility does not make material public domain. Academic attribution, copyright attribution, licence attribution, and quotation citation are separate records.

## Original-writing policy

All learner-facing prose must be newly written from the verified audit and outline. It must not preserve distinctive source sentences, paragraph order, headings beyond generic technical labels, examples, rhetorical sequence, or translation-equivalent expression.

Prohibit sentence-by-sentence close paraphrasing, cosmetic synonym replacement, translation as a workaround, and source text as hidden scaffolding where final wording remains substantially similar. The same verified concept may be explained in an independently designed structure and wording.

## Quotation policy

The production lesson should normally be quotation-free. Retain a quotation only when a necessary, documented citation purpose cannot be met by original explanation. Record the source, author, document, location, purpose, scope justification, and why original explanation is insufficient.

Keep any quotation as short as necessary, identify it clearly, and do not aggregate small quotations into a substitute for the source. Attribution is not a substitute for permission or citation-purpose analysis.

## Original-example policy

All teaching examples must be constructed examples. Create new objects, labels, contexts, numbers, and comparison structures; do not merely rename source objects while retaining their structure. Neutral `x`, `y`, and `z` labels are permitted to instantiate verified formal logic.

Mark examples internally as constructed, map them to verified theory, and check that they do not introduce utility, geometry, optimisation, or another excluded topic.

## Original-exercise and solution policy

Each future exercise must record its original prompt, objects or scenario, numerical or logical structure, answer choices where applicable, sequencing, solution, tested claim or objective, misconception, and confirmation that it was not copied or lightly transformed.

Do not adapt course exercises, textbooks, MIT problem sets, examinations, solution manuals, or question banks unless the exact asset licence permits the specific intended use and all conditions are satisfied. Any approved adaptation must be labelled `adapted` with attribution and licence information; no current exercise qualifies.

Every solution must be independently derived from verified definitions, explain the relevant reasoning, identify the definition or axiom supporting each important step, avoid source-solution wording and distinctive sequence, distinguish derivation from quotation, and be independently checked against the exercise.

## Table and notation policy

Create tables and notation summaries from scratch. Do not reproduce source wording, arrangement, category order, examples, visual hierarchy, captions, or distinctive information selection. Standard symbols and concise original definitions may appear in a newly designed table with academic citations.

## Graph and visual policy

No source graph, slide, diagram, screenshot, figure, or visual may be copied into production by default. Future visuals must be newly designed from verified concepts or original data, visually distinct, and have original labels and captions. Redrawing alone does not establish independence.

If a visual is intentionally adapted, inspect the exact licence; confirm commercial-use compatibility; provide attribution; identify changes; comply with ShareAlike, NonCommercial, and NoDerivatives terms; and reject it if incompatible with the platform’s intended use. No visual is required for the current verified subset.

## Citation and attribution policy

Every substantive academic claim must retain an academic citation to its verified basis. For statutory quotation or another exception, assess the specific rule and source-identification obligation separately. For licensed reuse, record licence, creator or attribution parties, copyright and licence notices if supplied, material URL, change indication, and any ShareAlike requirement.

Do not label independently written prose as MIT-authored or MIT-licensed merely because it cites MIT academically.

## AI-assisted drafting controls

Use only the verified audit and outline as substantive input. Do not place long source passages into production prompts, and instruct the drafting system not to imitate source wording or structure.

Before publication, a human reviewer must compare final prose with inspected sources for suspicious similarity, inspect distinctive phrases manually, verify formal claims against the audit, confirm examples and exercises are independent, and approve the result. AI generation does not guarantee originality, non-infringement, factual accuracy, or permission.

## Pre-publication review procedure

1. Confirm the planned material is within verified outline scope.
2. Confirm each substantive claim has an audit identifier and academic citation.
3. Confirm prose, examples, tables, exercises, and solutions are original rather than source-shaped.
4. Run a focused similarity review against inspected source phrasing and structures.
5. Verify exercise provenance, solution reasoning, and independent solution check.
6. Complete asset-specific rights review before any quotation, visual, adapted asset, or source material is included.
7. Record academic citation, copyright attribution, and licence attribution separately where applicable.
8. Confirm no material implies MIT, university, lecturer, or course endorsement.
9. Complete accessibility, editorial, and final publication review.
